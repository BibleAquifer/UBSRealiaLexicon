Description:
------------

All the words listed above are general terms for clothing. For a specific discussion on the clothing of the High Priest, see [4\.5 High Priest’s clothing\<REALIA:4\.5\>](#).

---

Translation:
------------

At times the context may indicate with some degree of certainty which specific article of clothing is intended. For the most part, however, a generic word for “clothing,” “clothes,” “dress,” or “covering” may be used.

Some have seen symbolic significance in the Hebrew word *’adereth* in [1KI 19:19](https://ref.ly/1Kgs19:19), when Elijah throws his mantle over Elisha. While this may be confirmed by the events of [2KI 2:8](https://ref.ly/2Kgs2:8); [2KI 2:13](https://ref.ly/2Kgs2:13); [2KI 2:14](https://ref.ly/2Kgs2:14), the translator should probably not use a different word than the one used in the other references for *’adereth*.

The Hebrew word *ksuth* means “covering” in the passages listed above.

The Hebrew word *pthigil* appears only in [ISA 3:24](https://ref.ly/Isa3:24), and its meaning is uncertain. The context makes it clear that it was some kind of fine garment that was probably wrapped around the wearer.

The Hebrew word *takrik* appears only in [EST 8:15](https://ref.ly/Esth8:15), and its root appears nowhere else in the Hebrew Bible. It was some sort of “mantle” (RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)), NJPSV (New Jewish Publication Society Version)), “cape” (CEV (Contemporary English Version)), or “cloak” (GNT (Good News Translation (1992))) that was wrapped around the outer garment. The one worn by Mordecai was a symbol of royalty like the rest of his clothes.

While the Greek word *esthēs* does not indicate a specific piece of clothing, it does refer to the high and impressive quality of the clothing. It is used several times for the clothing of angels. The Greek word *malakos* also refers to clothing of high quality.
