Description and usage:
----------------------

![Necklaces](../json/images/WEB-0533_necklace.jpg)

*Necklaces (© Wolfgang Sauber, CC BY\-SA 3\.0, via Wikimedia Commons)*

The necklace was a string, band, cord, or chain worn around the neck for ornamentation. Often jewels or other ornaments were attached to it.

---

Translation:
------------

[GEN 41:42](https://ref.ly/Gen41:42); [DAN 5:7](https://ref.ly/Dan5:7); [DAN 5:16](https://ref.ly/Dan5:16); [DAN 5:29](https://ref.ly/Dan5:29): In these passages a chain of gold is given by a ruler to someone who has been promoted to a high position. The chain in both contexts was probably more substantial than a simple necklace, and it will be better to use a word meaning “chain” rather than one referring to a thin decorative necklace. However, it is important to include in the translation something indicating honor or high rank and not slavery. In some languages a word exists for a symbol of honor or high office that is worn around the neck. Where such a word does not exist or is obscure, the element of honor may be included by expanding the translation; for example, GNT (Good News Translation (1992)) has “chain of honor” in [DAN 5:7](https://ref.ly/Dan5:7).

According to some commentators, the Aramaic word *hamnik* in [DAN 5:7](https://ref.ly/Dan5:7); [DAN 5:16](https://ref.ly/Dan5:16); [DAN 5:29](https://ref.ly/Dan5:29) does not actually refer to a chain but rather to a solid metal collar. NAB (New American Bible (1970)) has “collar.” In some languages a single verb can be used for wearing purple clothing and the golden collar as symbols of authority. But in others it will be more natural to use two separate verbs for “wearing” clothing and for “wearing” an ornament like a chain or collar around the neck.

[SNG 1:10](https://ref.ly/Song1:10): The Hebrew word *charuz* (plural, *charuzim*) actually means a small bead or gem with a hole in it. When many of these were stung onto a string, they formed a necklace. Some translations render *charuzim* as “necklaces” (NJB (New Jerusalem Bible (1985)), PV), while others prefer to speak about the “jewels” (GNT (Good News Translation (1992)), NCV (New Century Version)) or “precious stones” that made up the necklaces. Others combine the two: RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)) “strings of jewels”; NASB (New American Standard Bible) “strings of beads”; CEV (Contemporary English Version) “necklace of precious stones”; and SPCL (Spanish Common Language Version (Dios Habla Hoy)) “pearl necklaces.”

The meaning of the Hebrew word *kumaz* ([EXO 35:22](https://ref.ly/Exod35:22); [NUM 31:50](https://ref.ly/Num31:50)) is uncertain, but it clearly refers to some kind of jewelry. Most translations agree that it indicates something that was worn around the neck, whether “necklaces” (GNT (Good News Translation (1992)), GECL (German Common Language Version (Gute Nachricht Bibel))) or “pendants” (REB (Revised English Bible (1989)), NJPSV (New Jewish Publication Society Version)).

[GEN 38:18](https://ref.ly/Gen38:18); [GEN 38:25](https://ref.ly/Gen38:25): In these verses the Hebrew word *pathil* probably refers to nothing more than a simple cord around the neck, used to hold the signet ring. CEV (Contemporary English Version) is good with “cord around your neck” in [EXO 30:25](https://ref.ly/Exod30:25).

For the Hebrew words *netifah* and *saharonim*, see the discussion on [ISA 3:18–ISA 3:23](https://ref.ly/Isa3:18-Isa3:23) under [10\.5 Jewelry, ornaments\<REALIA:10\.5\>](#).
