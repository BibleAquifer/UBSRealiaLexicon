## Javelin (id: REALIA:2.6)

2\.6 Javelin
============

Description and usage:
----------------------

![Drawing of a javelin, or spear, used for hunting and fighting](../json/images/WEB-0955_javelin.jpg)

*Drawing of a javelin, or spear, used for hunting and fighting (© Unknown \- Wikimedia Commons)*

The javelin was a type of short spear or large arrow (see [2\.14\.2 Arrow\<REALIA:2\.14\.2\>](#)). It was lighter than the spear and, unlike the spear, was suited for throwing at the enemy from a standing position or while riding on a horse or in a chariot. Javelins were made of wood or reed. Like the spear, the javelin had a metal head or point fixed in the same way as a spearhead (see [2\.5\.1 Spearhead\<REALIA:2\.5\.1\>](#)). It often had a cord wrapped around its middle and looped over the finger of the thrower. As the javelin was released, the cord unwound, spinning the javelin and giving it greater stability, accuracy, and distance.

---

Translation:
------------

“Javelin” may be rendered “throwing spear” or “light spear.”

Roland de Vaux suggests that the Hebrew word *kidon* refers to a long sword or type of scimitar. This interpretation has been followed by GECL (German Common Language Version (Gute Nachricht Bibel)), CEV (Contemporary English Version) and NJB (New Jerusalem Bible (1985)) in some of the passages listed above. REB (Revised English Bible (1989)) has “sabre” at [JOB 41:21](https://ref.ly/Job41:21), but it shortens the weapon considerably at [JOS 8:26](https://ref.ly/Josh8:26) with “dagger.” Most translations, however, prefer “javelin,” “spear,” or “lance.”

The Hebrew word *shiryah* in [JOB 41:18](https://ref.ly/Job41:18) refers to a weapon, but it has not been definitely identified. Many translations have “javelin” (RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)), NIV (New International Version (1984))), GNT (Good News Translation (1992)) says “lance,” and NLT (New Living Translation) uses “pointed shaft.”

In [JHN 19:29](https://ref.ly/John19:29) the Greek word *hussos* is a variant reading that is accepted by some scholars (but rejected by most) instead of the better\-attested *hussōpos* (“hyssop”).
