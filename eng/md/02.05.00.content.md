Description:
------------

The spear was a long, straight rod with a sharp end used for piercing by thrusting. Its circumference was less than the grip of a man’s hand, and its length varied from the height of a man to much longer. It was normally made of wood with a metal head or point (see [2\.5\.1 Spearhead\<REALIA:2\.5\.1\>](#)). The butt of the spear was also covered with a pointed piece of metal (see [2SA 2:23](https://ref.ly/2Sam2:23)) and was used to stick the spear in the ground or to drive oxen or horses as a goad.

See the illustration at [2\.3\.1 Sheath, scabbard\<REALIA:2\.3\.1\>](#).

---

Usage:
------

In battle the spear was held in the middle and thrust at the enemy. Only rarely was it intended for throwing (see [2\.6 Javelin\<REALIA:2\.6\>](#)).

---

Translation:
------------

Where possible, translators should choose a word or phrase that indicates that this weapon was held and thrust (much like a knife on the end of a stick) rather than thrown.

The phrase “lifted up his spear” occurs in [2SA 23:18](https://ref.ly/2Sam23:18) and [1CH 11:11](https://ref.ly/1Chr11:11). GNT (Good News Translation (1992)) gives the correct meaning with “fought with his spear.”

The meaning of the Greek word *dexiolabos* in [ACT 23:23](https://ref.ly/Acts23:23) is uncertain. Most translations have “spearmen” (RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)), NIV (New International Version (1984))), “foot soldiers with spears” (CEV (Contemporary English Version)), “men with spears” (NCV (New Century Version)), or “light\-armed troops” (REB (Revised English Bible (1989))).
