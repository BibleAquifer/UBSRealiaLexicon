5\.17 Carpet, rug

Description and usage:
----------------------

![Carpets](images/WEB-0489_carpet.jpg)

*Carpets (© Grand Parc \- Bordeaux, France, CC BY 2\.0, via Wikimedia Commons)*

The carpet was a covering for the floor of a room. It could be made of skins or cloth, usually woven from the hair of animals.

---

Translation:
------------

[EZK 27:24](https://ref.ly/Ezek27:24): Like the other words in this entry, the Hebrew word *genez* may appear only once in the Bible. REB (Revised English Bible (1989)) understands it to mean “stores” (see [EST 3:9](https://ref.ly/Esth3:9); [EST 4:7](https://ref.ly/Esth4:7), and [3\.14\.1\.4 Treasury, strongroom\<REALIA:3\.14\.1\.4\>](#)), and GECL (German Common Language Version (Gute Nachricht Bibel)) has “garments,” but most translations agree that it means “carpets” (RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)), GNT (Good News Translation (1992))) or “rugs” (NIV (New International Version (1984))).

[JDG 5:10](https://ref.ly/Judg5:10): The meaning of the Hebrew word *mad* here is uncertain, so translations vary widely. A brief survey shows the following renderings: “rich carpets,” “carpets” (RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)), NRSV (New Revised Standard Version (1989)), ITCL (Italian Common Language Version), GECL (German Common Language Version (Gute Nachricht Bibel)), SPCL (Spanish Common Language Version (Dios Habla Hoy)), Luther, BE (Bible in Basic English)); “saddles,” “saddle\-cloths,” “saddle blankets” (GNT (Good News Translation (1992)), REB (Revised English Bible (1989)), NIV (New International Version (1984)), NCV (New Century Version)); “caparisons” (JB (Jerusalem Bible (1966))); “rich cloths” (the Jewish Publication Society’s *The Holy Scriptures*); “judgment” (Vulgate, KJV (King James Version (1611))). In this verse the speakers address a broad range of society, but it is unclear if they divide society into three groups (RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)) “you who ride on tawny asses, you who sit on rich carpets, and you who walk by the way”) or into two groups (CEV (Contemporary English Version) “Whether you ride on a donkey with a padded saddle or have to walk”). The decision on how many groups are in view depends on the meaning adopted for the Hebrew word *mad*. Since saddles were frequently made of a piece of cloth that closely resembled a floor rug, those riding on donkeys could be the same as those sitting on (a saddle made of) a carpet.

[ISA 21:5](https://ref.ly/Isa21:5): Some older translations (for example, KJV (King James Version (1611))) rendered the Hebrew word *tsafith* here as “watchtower.” It is now commonly accepted that it probably means a floor carpet on which people sat while eating.

[JDG 4:18](https://ref.ly/Judg4:18): The Hebrew word *smikah* appears only here in Scripture. It designates a thick cloth, perhaps a vertical hanging in a tent or a floor rug that could serve as a cover against the cold. GNT (Good News Translation (1992)) follows the first option by rendering the last clause in this verse as “she hid him behind a curtain.” RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)) selects the second option with “she covered him with a rug.” If the second option is chosen, the translator may want to follow the example of CEV (Contemporary English Version) by adding some implicit information here: “\[Sisera lay down, and] Jael covered him with a blanket.”

* **Associated Passages:** Ezekiel 27:24; Judges 5:10; Isaiah 21:5; Judges 4:18; Esther 3:9; Esther 4:7
