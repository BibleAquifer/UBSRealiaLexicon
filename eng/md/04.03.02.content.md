4\.3\.2 Temple: Sea, tank
=========================

Description:
------------

![Artist's drawing of the bronze Sea](../json/images/UBS-0026_bronze_sea.jpg)

*Artist's drawing of the bronze Sea (© Deutsche Bibelgesellschaft, Stuttgart by United Bible Societies)*

The Sea was a very large tank or pool for holding water. It was made of bronze and was 2\.5 meters (8 feet) deep, 5 meters (16\.5 feet) in diameter, and about 15 meters (49 feet) in circumference at its rim. Its sides were almost 8 centimeters (about 3 inches) thick. When empty, it would have weighed 25–30 tons or more. The two places where it is described differ on the amount of water it could hold; [1KI 7:26](https://ref.ly/1Kgs7:26) indicates 38,000 liters (10,000 gallons), while [2CH 4:5](https://ref.ly/2Chr4:5) has 57,000 liters (15,000 gallons). It is not clear from the texts whether the Sea was shaped like a bowl (hemisphere) or a cylinder standing on end. According to [1KI 7:24](https://ref.ly/1Kgs7:24) and [2CH 4:3](https://ref.ly/2Chr4:3), around its outer edge were two rows of bronze gourds (NRSV (New Revised Standard Version (1989)) “panels”; NCV (New Century Version) “plants”) as decoration. The “legs” of the Sea were 12 oxen, also made of bronze, in four sets of three each. They faced outward, and the Sea rested on their backs. [2CH 4:6](https://ref.ly/2Chr4:6) states that the water was used by the priests for washing, but details are lacking.

---

Translation:
------------

In some languages it will be best to use a descriptive phrase for “Sea”; compare NCV (New Century Version), which renders it “large round bowl, which was called the Sea” in [1KI 7:23](https://ref.ly/1Kgs7:23). While this basin is called *yam* (“Sea”) in Hebrew, there is no reason why a translation needs to retain this metaphorical name.
