Description and usage:
----------------------

![Drawing of a wagon model](images/UBS-0035_wagon_model.jpg)

*Drawing of a wagon model (© Deutsche Bibelgesellschaft, Stuttgart by United Bible Societies)*

The wagon was a two or four\-wheeled carriage or cart used for travel or the transport of loads. Such vehicles were usually made of wood. They were pulled by draft animals, usually oxen, donkeys, or horses. These animals were attached to a long pole that was in turn attached to the front of the wagon. The upper section of the wagon was often formed by crossed wooden bars in the shape of a cage.

---

Translation:
------------

Wagons and carts in the Bible were always pulled by animals. Translators should avoid any word for “wagon” that indicates a vehicle propelled by an engine.

![
            ](images/OAI-0066_cart_with_yoke_of_oxen.png)

*(Image generated by ChatGPT using OpenAI technology)*

[NUM 7:3](https://ref.ly/Num7:3): The meaning of the Hebrew phrase *‘egloth tsav* here is uncertain. Most translators and commentators say something like “covered wagons” (RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)), REB (Revised English Bible (1989))). These would be wagons as described above with a cloth covering the items carried in each wagon. Such a wagon might resemble a tortoise or turtle, which is a meaning of the Hebrew word *tsav*.

[EZK 23:24](https://ref.ly/Ezek23:24); [EZK 26:10](https://ref.ly/Ezek26:10): The Hebrew word *galgal* is literally “wheel.” In the context of these verses, it is clear that this is a metonym for a vehicle with wheels, that is, a cart or wagon.

[1ES 5:53](https://ref.ly/1Esd5:53): In this verse some Greek manuscripts have the word *chara* (“joy”), which makes no sense in this context. One manuscript has the word *karron*, meaning “wagon” as described above.
