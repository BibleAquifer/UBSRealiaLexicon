Description and usage:
----------------------

![A stone fence protected sheep at night](images/WEB-0930_sheepfold.jpg)

*A stone fence protected sheep at night (© Free Bible Images © David Padfield)*

The sheep pen was an area enclosed by a stone wall or hedge made of branches and thorns. It had a narrow opening through which the sheep could pass and which could be closed with brush or by the shepherd’s body. Sheep were put in the pen at night to protect them from thieves or wild animals.

---

Translation:
------------

“Sheep pen” must often be rendered “place for the protection of sheep,” “place for sheep with a wall built around it,” or “enclosure for sheep.” In contrast to the next entry ([1\.2\.2 Animal pen, stall\<REALIA:1\.2\.2\>](#)), this enclosure was built out in the areas where the sheep were grazing, and not near the house. It could often be a temporary structure.

In [JER 49:3](https://ref.ly/Jer49:3) the Hebrew word *gderoth* is rendered “sheep\-pens” by NJB (New Jerusalem Bible (1985)) and “sheepfolds” by NJPSV (New Jewish Publication Society Version). Others have “walls” (NIV (New International Version (1984)), NASB (New American Standard Bible), New Century Version \[NCV (New Century Version)]) or “hedges” (RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)), New Living Translation \[NLT (New Living Translation)]). Others, however, prefer a small change in the Hebrew, so that it would mean “covered with gashes” as an expression of mourning. Translations that take this approach render the last half of the fourth line in this verse as “lament, and slash yourselves with whips” (NRSV (New Revised Standard Version (1989))) or “and score your bodies with gashes” (Revised English Bible \[REB (Revised English Bible (1989))]).

The Hebrew words *mishpthayim* and *shfatayim* are translated in different ways. In [GEN 49:14](https://ref.ly/Gen49:14)RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)) and NJPSV (New Jewish Publication Society Version) have “sheepfolds,” while REB (Revised English Bible (1989)) says “cattle pens.” These versions believe these words refer to an enclosure for domestic animals. GNT (Good News Translation (1992)) and NIV (New International Version (1984)) have “saddlebags,” which is preferred by *A Handbook on Genesis*, page 1090\.
