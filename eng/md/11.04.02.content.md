## Measures of length in the New Testament and the Deuterocanon (id: REALIA:11.4.2)

11\.4\.2 Measures of length in the New Testament and the Deuterocanon
=====================================================================



## Pēchus (id: REALIA:11.4.2.1)

11\.4\.2\.1 Pēchus
==================

References:
-----------

Greek πῆχυς (pēchus)

[MAT 6:27](https://ref.ly/Matt6:27), [LUK 12:25](https://ref.ly/Luke12:25), [JHN 21:8](https://ref.ly/John21:8), [REV 21:17](https://ref.ly/Rev21:17), [JDT 1:2](https://ref.ly/Jdt1:2), [JDT 1:2](https://ref.ly/Jdt1:2), [JDT 1:2](https://ref.ly/Jdt1:2), [JDT 1:2](https://ref.ly/Jdt1:2), [JDT 1:3](https://ref.ly/Jdt1:3), [JDT 1:3](https://ref.ly/Jdt1:3), [JDT 1:4](https://ref.ly/Jdt1:4), [JDT 1:4](https://ref.ly/Jdt1:4), [2MA 13:5](https://ref.ly/2Macc13:5), [1ES 6:24](https://ref.ly/1Esd6:24), [1ES 6:24](https://ref.ly/1Esd6:24), [S3Y 1:24](https://ref.ly/PrAzar1:24)

The *pēchus* is the New Testament equivalent of the cubit (*’amah* in Hebrew).
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

In most cases the translator will be able to avoid awkward fractions and decimal points for the *pēchus* by using the formula, two cubits equal one meter or one yard.

[MAT 6:27](https://ref.ly/Matt6:27); [LUK 12:25](https://ref.ly/Luke12:25): One problem of interpretation here is that the Greek word *pēchus* is normally used of distance rather than of time. However, it may also be used of time, and that meaning is much more satisfactory in the present context. Moreover, the Greek word rendered “span of life” by RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)) is more frequently used as a measurement of time than height. Modern translations tend to accept the meaning of a measure of time. If span is used as a measurement of time, the use of the word *pēchus* to qualify it would mean a brief span, whereas if it is used of a person’s height, it would be a significant amount (about half a meter or 18 inches). NAB (New American Bible (1970)) renders the last part of [MAT 6:27](https://ref.ly/Matt6:27) as “add a moment to his life\-span,” and GECL (German Common Language Version (Gute Nachricht Bibel)) has “lengthen his life only by one day.” Other possibilities are “add a little time to his life,” “live a little longer,” and “make his life last even a little longer.” Some translators will indicate a specific amount of time, such as “even one day” or “even one hour.”

[JHN 21:8](https://ref.ly/John21:8): The distance given here is approximately 100 meters or 100 yards. Translators will want to follow something like CEV (Contemporary English Version) “only about a hundred yards from shore.”

[REV 21:17](https://ref.ly/Rev21:17): The text gives a measurement of 144 cubits, that is, twelve times twelve. This seems to be a symbolic number, and many translations—among them common\-language translations such as GW (God's Word Translation)NCV (New Century Version)ITCL (Italian Common Language Version)GECL (German Common Language Version (Gute Nachricht Bibel)) —choose to leave the number in the text. ITCL (Italian Common Language Version) also adds the metric equivalent in the text, saying “one hundred forty\-four cubits (seventy meters).”

GNT (Good News Translation (1992)) takes this measurement, 144 cubits (72 meters; 236 feet), to be the height of the wall (also NJB (New Jerusalem Bible (1985))REB (Revised English Bible (1989))FRCL (French Common Language Version (Bible en français courant)), Phps (J.B. Phillips: The New Testament in Modern English (1958))). It may, however, refer to its thickness (NIV (New International Version (1984))BRCL (Brazilian Common Language Bible)). Some commentators make the point that, for a city that is 1,500 miles tall, a wall only 72 meters (236 feet) tall is so small that it seems foolish. But it is also pointed out that the wall, in this case, is not for the protection of the city (inasmuch as the gates stay open all the time) but for its demarcation. In any case, the notion of height is preferable to that of thickness. A translation should opt for one or the other; simply to say, as RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)) and others say, “a hundred and forty\-four cubits” (or, “72 meters” or “236 feet”), without indicating that this is the height, does not qualify as a translation. An alternative translation model for the first half of this verse is “He also determined the height \[or, thickness] of the wall \[or, fence]. It was 72 meters \[or, 236 feet].” For this verse see also *A Handbook on The Revelation to John*, pages 306–307\.

* **Associated Passages:** Matthew 6:27; Luke 12:25; John 21:8; Revelation 21:17; Judith 1:2; Judith 1:3; Judith 1:4; 2 Maccabees 13:5; 1 Esdras (Greek) 6:24; Song of the 3 Young Men 1:24


## Fathom (id: REALIA:11.4.2.2)

11\.4\.2\.2 Fathom
==================

References:
-----------

Greek ὀργυιά (orguia)

[ACT 27:28](https://ref.ly/Acts27:28), [ACT 27:28](https://ref.ly/Acts27:28)

[ACT 27:28](https://ref.ly/Acts27:28); [ACT 27:28](https://ref.ly/Acts27:28): The *orguia* was traditionally the measure of a man’s outstretched arms from fingertip to fingertip. It was reckoned at approximately 6 feet or about 1\.85 meters. It was used as a technical nautical term to measure the depth of water. Water depth was measured with rope with a weight on the end, and this measure possibly derives from the way of counting off the arm lengths of rope. The measurements mentioned in this verse are about 40 meters (130 feet) and about 30 meters (100 feet). Where a language has a unit of measure that is used specifically for water depth (like the English word “fathom”), it may be used here.
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

* **Associated Passages:** Acts 27:28

* **Associated ACAI Concepts:** Fathom (ID: `realia:Fathom`)

## Stadium, furlong (id: REALIA:11.4.2.3)

11\.4\.2\.3 Stadium, furlong
============================

References:
-----------

Greek στάδιον (stadion)

[2MA 11:5](https://ref.ly/2Macc11:5), [2MA 12:9](https://ref.ly/2Macc12:9), [2MA 12:10](https://ref.ly/2Macc12:10), [2MA 12:16](https://ref.ly/2Macc12:16), [2MA 12:17](https://ref.ly/2Macc12:17), [2MA 12:29](https://ref.ly/2Macc12:29)

Greek στάδιος (stadios)

[MAT 14:24](https://ref.ly/Matt14:24), [LUK 24:13](https://ref.ly/Luke24:13), [JHN 6:19](https://ref.ly/John6:19), [JHN 11:18](https://ref.ly/John11:18), [REV 14:20](https://ref.ly/Rev14:20), [REV 21:16](https://ref.ly/Rev21:16)

[MAT 14:24](https://ref.ly/Matt14:24): The Greek says that the boat was “many stadia” from land. It is not necessary to express this in any particular unit of measurement. GNT (Good News Translation (1992)) “the boat was far out in the lake” is a good model. (Some Greek manuscripts have “in the middle of the lake” instead of “many stadia from the land.” In either case, neither reading gives a specific measurement; both merely state that the boat was now some distance from the shore, and therefore any phrase that conveys this information will be faithful to the intent of the text.)
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[LUK 24:13](https://ref.ly/Luke24:13): The distance given here in the best manuscripts is “sixty stadia,” which is about 11 kilometers (7 miles). Some manuscripts have “one hundred sixty stadia,” but this reading cannot be correct. Other ways of expressing “sixty stadia” might be “two hour’s walk” (Leyden, Sranan, Timorese) and “two leagues” (Tzeltal, where “a league” is commonly explained as “one hour’s walk”).

[JHN 6:19](https://ref.ly/John6:19): English translations generally give “three or four miles” (RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952))). Where the metric system is used, you would say “five or six kilometers” (FRCL (French Common Language Version (Bible en français courant))).

[JHN 11:18](https://ref.ly/John11:18): In this verse translations generally have “about two miles” (RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952))) or “almost two miles.” Where the metric system is used, you would say “about three kilometers.” It would also be possible to say “about a half hour walk.”

[REV 14:20](https://ref.ly/Rev14:20); [REV 21:16](https://ref.ly/Rev21:16): The numbers in these verses, 1,600 stadia and 12,000 stadia respectively, are probably symbolic and, as such, are more important than the exact distance they might represent. For this reason some translations prefer to retain the numbers and explain the distances in a note or in parentheses in the text. So, for example, ITCL (Italian Common Language Version) has “twelve thousand stadia (more than two thousand kilometers),” while NIV (New International Version (1984)) says “12,000 stadia in length” and adds this footnote: “That is, about 1,400 miles (about 2,200 kilometers).”

* **Associated Passages:** 2 Maccabees 11:5; 2 Maccabees 12:9; 2 Maccabees 12:10; 2 Maccabees 12:16; 2 Maccabees 12:17; 2 Maccabees 12:29; Matthew 14:24; Luke 24:13; John 6:19; John 11:18; Revelation 14:20; Revelation 21:16


## Sabbath day’s journey (id: REALIA:11.4.2.4)

11\.4\.2\.4 Sabbath day’s journey
=================================

References:
-----------

Greek σάββατον ἔχον ὁδόν (sabbaton echon hodon)

[ACT 1:12](https://ref.ly/Acts1:12)

[ACT 1:12](https://ref.ly/Acts1:12): Jewish sources approximately contemporary with the New Testament period defined “a Sabbath day’s journey” as 2,000 cubits, which is about one kilometer or 1,000 yards. Some translations express this as “about half a mile” (GNT (Good News Translation (1992))) or “about a kilometer” (PV). ITCL (Italian Common Language Version) renders it in terms of the amount of walking time, “a half hour’s walk.” SPCL (Spanish Common Language Version (Dios Habla Hoy)) expands it to “exactly that which the law permitted one to walk on the day of rest,” although this may be felt to be too cumbersome. Also, this rendering still does not tell the reader what distance was involved.

## Mile (id: REALIA:11.4.2.5)

11\.4\.2\.5 Mile
================

References:
-----------

Greek μίλιον (milion)

[MAT 5:41](https://ref.ly/Matt5:41)

[MAT 5:41](https://ref.ly/Matt5:41): There are a number of ways in which the distances can be expressed here. Many translations have maintained the literal mile, even in situations where the metric system is employed. “One mile” was presumably the distance a Roman soldier could force a Jew to carry his equipment. What is important here is not the exact distance, but the relation between “one mile” and “two miles.” While this relation will be clear even in settings where miles are unknown, many translations choose to say “one kilometer” and “two kilometers” simply to avoid introducing the unknown word, “mile.”
----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Actually, it is possible to substitute any distance that takes about 15–20 minutes to walk, or even to express it in walking time; compare the Rincon Zapotec rendering for this verse: “If anyone forces you to carry his load for half an hour, go with this person for a whole hour.” In some cultures it may be natural to express the “mile” in terms of its original meaning in Latin, which is “a thousand paces.”

* **Associated Passages:** Matthew 5:41

* **Associated ACAI Concepts:** Mile (ID: `realia:Mile`)

## Schoinos (id: REALIA:11.4.2.6)

11\.4\.2\.6 Schoinos
====================

References:
-----------

Greek σχοῖνος (schoinos)

[2MA 11:5](https://ref.ly/2Macc11:5)

The *schoinos* was a distance equal to thirty stadia, about 5\.6 kilometers (3\.5 miles). There is a textual problem in [2MA 11:5](https://ref.ly/2Macc11:5), which seems to have read “five stadia,” which is much too close to Jerusalem. Already in ancient times the text was emended to read “five *schoinos*.”

* **Associated Passages:** 2 Maccabees 11:5
