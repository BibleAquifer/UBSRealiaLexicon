Translation:
------------

Generally, the words listed above carry a range of usages similar to that of the English word “bag.” These words refer to bags that can vary in size, in material, and in quality. Thus they indicate sacks for carrying grain but also handbags carried by women. Often a generic word for a bag made of pliable material such as cloth or leather will suffice. Translators should, of course, avoid a word that specifically indicates a bag made of plastic or other modern synthetic material. The context will usually help the translator choose between specialized terms, where such exist; for example, the Hebrew word *charitim* in [2KI 5:23](https://ref.ly/2Kgs5:23) may be rendered “bags” or even “sacks,” while the same word in [ISA 3:22](https://ref.ly/Isa3:22) is better translated “purses” or “handbags.”

In [GEN 42:0](https://ref.ly/Gen42:0) – [GEN 44:0](https://ref.ly/Gen44:0) the Hebrew word *’amtachath* indicates a large bag for carrying grain.

[DEU 25:13](https://ref.ly/Deut25:13); [PRO 16:11](https://ref.ly/Prov16:11); [MIC 6:11](https://ref.ly/Mic6:11): In these verses the Hebrew word *kis* refers to a bag in which a merchant carried weights. The point of these verses is that business dealings must always be honest, and the fact that the weights were kept in a bag is insignificant. For this reason GNT (Good News Translation (1992)) renders [DEU 25:13](https://ref.ly/Deut25:13) (literally “You shall not have in your bag a stone and a stone, large and small”) as “Do not cheat when you use weights \[and measures].” The same approach is taken for the other two references. It is a good model.

In some passages it will be possible to replace the word “purse” with its contents, “money.” The second line of [PRO 16:11](https://ref.ly/Prov16:11) is rendered by GECL (German Common Language Version (Gute Nachricht Bibel)) in a similar way, where it has “We will share the booty.” GNT (Good News Translation (1992)) says “we’ll all share what we steal.”

The Hebrew word *tsror* may usually be understood as “bundle,” that is, a wrapping prepared for the occasion and not so much a ready container or bag.

The Greek word *zōnē* in [MAT 10:9](https://ref.ly/Matt10:9) and [MRK 6:8](https://ref.ly/Mark6:8) (RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)) “belts”) refers to the place where a man usually carried his money. GNT (Good News Translation (1992)) “pockets” provides a cultural equivalent, and so does NJB (New Jerusalem Bible (1985)) with “purses.” NAB (New American Bible (1970)) retains “belts,” while Brc says “money\-belts.” Some translators will use a general expression, such as “money carriers” or “things where you carry your money.” However, more often this object is dropped altogether; for example, the last clause of [MAT 10:9](https://ref.ly/Matt10:9) may be rendered “carry no money with you.”

[MAT 10:10](https://ref.ly/Matt10:10); [MRK 6:8](https://ref.ly/Mark6:8); [LUK 9:3](https://ref.ly/Luke9:3): In these verses the Greek word *pēra* is translated many different ways: “beggar’s bag (GNT (Good News Translation (1992))), “beggar’s knapsack” (Brc), “haversack” (NJB (New Jerusalem Bible (1985))), “pack” (REB (Revised English Bible (1989))), “traveling bag” (NAB (New American Bible (1970))), “knapsack” (Phps (J.B. Phillips: The New Testament in Modern English (1958))), “bag” (RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)), AT (American Translation (Goodspeed, 1935))), and “wallet” (Mft (Moffatt Translation (1926))). The problem is that this word in the New Testament may refer in general to any sort of bag that a traveler may put his things in or it may have the specialized sense of a bag carried by a beggar. As the survey of translations suggests, there is no agreement among scholars. Therefore translators can say either “bag for travelers” or “bag carried by beggars.” Obviously the apostles were to carry nothing with them when they went out.
