Description and usage:
----------------------

![Tomb with niches, with wall painting of man playing a flute and woman playing a harp (Beit Guvrin)](../json/images/WEB-0467_burial.jpg)

*Tomb with niches, with wall painting of man playing a flute and woman playing a harp (Beit Guvrin) (© Carole Raddato from FRANKFURT, Germany, CC BY\-SA 2\.0, via Wikimedia Commons)*

The tomb was a construction for the burial of the dead. Since much of the land of Israel, especially in the hilly regions, has little topsoil, tombs were often hewn out of bedrock. In some cases natural caves were used for burial. The entrance to a tomb was closed with a stone (see [4\.8\.1\.1 Stone for closing a tomb\<REALIA:4\.8\.1\.1\>](#)).

---

Translation:
------------

Death is a universal experience, and all languages and cultures have words to express the disposal of a dead body. In a number of languages it is important to distinguish clearly between a grave (a hole in the ground in which a corpse is placed) and a tomb (a construction built above the surface of the ground for housing one or more corpses). In some contexts, however, it may be better to use a more generic expression, for example, “places where people are buried” or “places where corpses are placed.”

In some passages the word “grave” is used as a symbol for death. Translators should consider whether the physical burial place is in focus or rather the fact that a person has died. In the latter case, it may be better to use a term indicating “death.” This will be true especially in poetic passages. For example, [JOB 3:22](https://ref.ly/Job3:22) is literally “who rejoicing unto joy are glad when they find a grave,” but GNT (Good News Translation (1992)) has “They are not happy till they are dead and buried.” [JOB 5:26](https://ref.ly/Job5:26) is literally “You will come to the grave at an old age,” but GNT (Good News Translation (1992)) says “you will live to a ripe old age.”

Certain verses speak of “graves” in the plural. Here it will often be appropriate to use a word like “graveyard” (GNT (Good News Translation (1992)) in [JOB 21:32](https://ref.ly/Job21:32)) or “cemetery.”

In the upper levels of Israelite society burial was often done in two stages. In the first stage the body was either laid on a kind of shelf inside a burial chamber or it was placed in a large box called a sarcophagus (“flesh eater”) in English. After about a year, when the flesh had rotted, the bones were moved to a designated place in the chamber. By New Testament times the bones were being placed into an ossuary (“bone box”). These practices gave rise to two expressions in the Hebrew language: “he lay down with his fathers” and “he was gathered to his fathers.” In [2CH 16:14](https://ref.ly/2Chr16:14), the place where the body was laid out (*mishkav* in Hebrew) is referred to in the burial of King Asa of Judah. Some translations render the word as “bier” (RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)), NIV (New International Version (1984)); see [4\.8\.4 Bier, coffin\<REALIA:4\.8\.4\>](#)), but the structure of the verse indicates that Asa’s body was taken into the tomb and laid in its permanent place, that is, on a shelf in the tomb. The rendering of NJPSV (New Jewish Publication Society Version) gives the sense better with “resting\-place.”

[1SA 13:6](https://ref.ly/1Sam13:6): The meaning of the Hebrew word *tsrichim* in this verse is uncertain. Some versions understand it to mean “tombs” (RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)), CEV (Contemporary English Version), GECL (German Common Language Version (Gute Nachricht Bibel)), ITCL (Italian Common Language Version)). These would be cave\-like structures hewn out of the rock. Others use a more generic term, such as “pits” (GNT (Good News Translation (1992)), NIV (New International Version (1984))) or “vaults” (NJB (New Jerusalem Bible (1985))). Some have thought this word is related to a Hebrew word meaning “stronghold” or “tower” in [JDG 9:46](https://ref.ly/Judg9:46), [JDG 9:49](https://ref.ly/Judg9:49), but this meaning does not seem to fit the context here.

In [MAT 23:29](https://ref.ly/Matt23:29) the Greek word *mnēmeion* may also be understood as referring to monuments built as memorials to people who have died. Here it may be translated “monuments to the dead.” This is also its meaning in [LUK 11:47](https://ref.ly/Luke11:47). The Latin word *monumentum* in [2ES 2:16](https://ref.ly/2Esd2:16) may be understood in the same way.

In [MRK 5:2](https://ref.ly/Mark5:2) it is important to avoid a term that would designate merely graves, for the demoniac who met Jesus was not living down in graves in the ground, but had found some habitation either in burial caves or in tombs constructed above ground.

## Stone for closing a tomb (id: REALIA:4.8.1.1)

4\.8\.1\.1 Stone for closing a tomb
===================================

References:
-----------

Greek λίθος (lithos)

[MAT 27:60](https://ref.ly/Matt27:60), [MAT 27:66](https://ref.ly/Matt27:66), [MAT 28:2](https://ref.ly/Matt28:2), [MRK 15:46](https://ref.ly/Mark15:46), [MRK 16:3](https://ref.ly/Mark16:3), [MRK 16:4](https://ref.ly/Mark16:4), [LUK 24:2](https://ref.ly/Luke24:2), [JHN 11:39](https://ref.ly/John11:39), [JHN 11:41](https://ref.ly/John11:41), [JHN 20:1](https://ref.ly/John20:1)

Description and usage:
----------------------

![Round stone for sealing a tomb entrance](../json/images/WEB-0468_stone_closure.jpg)

*Round stone for sealing a tomb entrance (© Ian Scott, CC BY\-SA 2\.0, via Wikimedia Commons)*

A large, heavy stone was used to close the entrance to a cave or tomb cut out of the rock.

---

Translation:
------------

Two different methods were used to close tombs with stones, and it is unclear from the Gospels which method is described in the accounts of Jesus’ burial. In one method the stone was cylindrical. It sat on its side in a channel the same width as the stone, and it was pushed sideways until it covered the mouth of the tomb. By the second method the entrance to the tomb was reached by going down a short incline. The stone was spherical and was rolled down the incline until it plugged the tomb entrance.

[JHN 11:39](https://ref.ly/John11:39); [JHN 11:39](https://ref.ly/John11:39); [JHN 11:41](https://ref.ly/John11:41): The place where Lazarus was buried is described as a cave with a stone placed at the entrance. In [JHN 11:38](https://ref.ly/John11:38) the Greek phrase that GNT (Good News Translation (1992)) renders “at the entrance” is ambiguous. It may mean “on it,” in which case the shaft of the burial tomb was vertical. Or it may mean “against it,” in which case the shaft was horizontal. Commentators are divided in their opinions, but the weight of evidence seems to favor a horizontal cave. In either case, the purpose of the stone was primarily to keep animals from entering the tomb and devouring the body. “A stone placed at the entrance” (GNT (Good News Translation (1992))) may often be best translated “a stone covering the opening to the cave” or “a large stone covering the hole of the cave.” Note that the text does not state here, as in the case of Jesus’ burial, that the stone was “rolled” in front of the entrance.

* **Associated Passages:** Matthew 27:60; Matthew 27:66; Matthew 28:2; Mark 15:46; Mark 16:3; Mark 16:4; Luke 24:2; John 11:39; John 11:41; John 20:1; John 11:38

Most translations use a word for “tomb” and add a footnote giving the alternate reading (so GNT (Good News Translation (1992))).

## Embalming oils (id: REALIA:4.8.3.1)

4\.8\.3\.1 Embalming oils
=========================

References:
-----------

Greek ἀλόη (aloē)

[JHN 19:39](https://ref.ly/John19:39)

Greek ἄρωμα (arōma)

[MRK 16:1](https://ref.ly/Mark16:1), [LUK 23:56](https://ref.ly/Luke23:56), [LUK 24:1](https://ref.ly/Luke24:1), [JHN 19:40](https://ref.ly/John19:40)

Greek μύρον (muron)

[LUK 23:56](https://ref.ly/Luke23:56)

Greek σμύρνα (smurna)

[JHN 19:39](https://ref.ly/John19:39)

Description and usage:
----------------------

Embalming oils were aromatic oils or salves used especially in preparing a dead body for burial in a way that it would be preserved. See also [10\.6\.1\.1 Myrrh\<REALIA:10\.6\.1\.1\>](#).

---

Translation:
------------

In [2CH 16:14](https://ref.ly/2Chr16:14) we find the Hebrew expression *bsamim uznim mruqachim* in the context of the burial of King Asa of Judah. The text says that spices and aromatic oils were placed in the tomb (CEV (Contemporary English Version) “the tomb was filled with spices and sweet\-smelling oils”). There is no reason to assume that Asa’s body was embalmed (like Jacob’s body in [GEN 50:2](https://ref.ly/Gen50:2); [GEN 50:3](https://ref.ly/Gen50:3)), although GNT (Good News Translation (1992)) comes close with “They used spices and perfumed oils to prepare his body for burial.”

In [LUK 23:56](https://ref.ly/Luke23:56) translators may use a descriptive phrase to render the Greek word *muron*, for example, “perfumed oil for preserving the body” or “… to keep the body from rotting.”

Ingredients mentioned in the New Testament for embalming oils are “myrrh” (*smurna* in Greek) and “aloes” (*aloē* in Greek). Most translators borrow terms for these ingredients and then use some kind of marginal note to explain the nature of their substance.

* **Associated Passages:** John 19:39; Mark 16:1; Luke 23:56; Luke 24:1; John 19:40; 2 Chronicles 16:14; Genesis 50:2; Genesis 50:3

* **Associated ACAI Concepts:** Aloes (ID: `realia:Aloes`)

## Bier, coffin (id: REALIA:4.8.4)

4\.8\.4 Bier, coffin
====================

References:
-----------

Hebrew אֲרוֹן (’aron)

[GEN 50:26](https://ref.ly/Gen50:26)

Hebrew מִטָּה (mitah)

[2SA 3:31](https://ref.ly/2Sam3:31)

Greek σορός (soros)

[LUK 7:14](https://ref.ly/Luke7:14)

Description and usage:
----------------------

![Stone coffins or ossuaries in the garden of Dominus Flevit on the Mount of Olives](../json/images/WEB-0470_coffin.jpg)

*Stone coffins or ossuaries in the garden of Dominus Flevit on the Mount of Olives (© Deror Avi, CC BY\-SA 3\.0, via Wikimedia Commons)*

The bier was a board on which a body was carried to its place of burial, and the coffin was a box in which a body was placed for burial.

---

Translation:
------------

In [GEN 50:26](https://ref.ly/Gen50:26) the Hebrew word *’aron* is literally “box,” but it means “coffin” in this context.

In [2SA 3:31](https://ref.ly/2Sam3:31) the Hebrew word *mitah* is literally “bed.” The context, however, makes it clear that the object is serving as a bier or, less likely, a coffin.

[LUK 7:14](https://ref.ly/Luke7:14): In this verse some translations render the Greek word *soros* as “coffin” (GNT (Good News Translation (1992)), NCV (New Century Version)). This word can also refer to a “bier,” and that is its more probable meaning here. In some languages it may be necessary to use a descriptive expression, for example, “board on which the body was being carried” or “box in which the body lay.”

* **Associated Passages:** Genesis 50:26; 2 Samuel 3:31; Luke 7:14

Description and usage:
----------------------

Sackcloth was a coarse dark material made of goat or camel hair. It was worn next to the skin as a sign of mourning or repentance ([2KI 6:30](https://ref.ly/2Kgs6:30)).

---

Translation:
------------

Customs associated with mourning vary greatly in different cultures, and in some the clothing or lack of clothing is not relevant. In most languages “sackcloth” is described as “coarse cloth” and sometimes as “heavy cloth.” It will often be best to clarify the relation between these clothes and mourning; for example, “clothes that show someone is mourning for the dead.”

In some languages there may exist an equivalent symbol of mourning, such as body painting, smearing ashes over the face or chest, carrying objects such as bags symbolizing a needy widow’s mourning, or wearing a cord of mourning around the neck. There are dangers as well as advantages in such a cultural translation. Particular aspects of the Israelite culture are lost, and there may be undesirable meanings associated with the local practice. Whenever this is done, all of its ramifications should be checked carefully. Generally it will be preferable to use the Israelite custom, with a footnote explaining its meaning or providing the equivalent custom. A footnote might read “Mourning clothes were coarse pieces of cloth sewed together from woven goat or camel hair.”

[2SA 14:2](https://ref.ly/2Sam14:2) does not specifically mention sackcloth but speaks of “mourning clothes.” It was the Jewish custom to tear the outer garment as a sign of despair or sadness. However, this verse indicates something more than just tearing the clothing. For the literal clause “put on mourning clothes,” CEV (Contemporary English Version) and NCV (New Century Version) have “Put on funeral clothes.” Where no special custom is known, or where a known one would be inappropriate for the context of this story, it may be possible to expand this clause to “Go and put on special clothes that will make David think you are sad because someone has died.”

[JOB 16:15](https://ref.ly/Job16:15): The first half of this verse is literally “I have sewed sackcloth upon my skin” (so RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952))). This may suggest that Job will wear the sackcloth permanently, or that he wears it next to his skin. GNT (Good News Translation (1992)) “I mourn and wear clothes made of sackcloth” avoids expressing either suggestion. GECL (German Common Language Version (Gute Nachricht Bibel)), however, translates “The mourning clothes are my second skin,” which implies that Job will wear them all the time. This line may be rendered “I have put mourning clothes on my body,” “I have dressed myself in the clothes of those who mourn for the dead,” or “I wear the rough cloth of those who are in mourning.”

In some passages the wearing of sackcloth is not an act of mourning because of death but rather an expression of sorrow and penitence. See, for example, [ISA 58:5](https://ref.ly/Isa58:5); [DAN 9:3](https://ref.ly/Dan9:3); [JON 3:6](https://ref.ly/Jonah3:6).

[MAT 11:21](https://ref.ly/Matt11:21): The last part of this verse is literally “they would have repented long ago in sackcloth and ashes” (so RSV (Revised Standard Version (1952)); see also [LUK 10:13](https://ref.ly/Luke10:13)). Instead of retaining the reference to putting on “sackcloth and ashes,” some translations have said simply “long ago they would have shown to everyone that they were turning from their sins.” However, translators should try to retain these cultural actions in the text, since they were a common aspect of biblical culture.
